Climate warming is advancing plant phenology faster than the date of the last spring freeze, expanding the window of vulnerability to cold damage. In early spring, a progressive loss of cold hardiness with phenological development leaves emerging tissues exposed to freezing events — a risk that is growing as warming reshapes the seasonal calendar.

In this collaborative review, we synthesized freeze damage thresholds across row crops, fruit trees, and forest species, identifying key discrepancies between empirically measured and model-derived thresholds. Our analysis shows that model-based studies consistently report higher (less-hardy) thresholds compared to empirical measurements, highlighting the need to account for microclimatic factors (humidity, topography, wind speed) and physiological considerations (phenology and species) to improve freeze risk predictions in the field.
