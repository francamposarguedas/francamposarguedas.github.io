During my Master's at Université Laval and Université Sainte-Anne, I studied how temperature shapes berry maturation and the accumulation of volatile in grapevines grown in eastern Canada. Volatile compounds in grapevine berries play a central role in wine quality, yet they are highly sensitive to environmental conditions, making them particularly vulnerable to the temperature shifts associated with global warming.

Using on-the-row mini-greenhouses in an experimental vineyard in Nova Scotia, we manipulated temperature and UV exposure in *Vitis* sp. cv. L'Acadie blanc at different stages of berry development, from pre-veraison through full maturity, to understand how the timing of temperature increases affects berry composition at harvest. Berries exposed to warmer conditions during early development accumulated higher concentrations of certain benzene derivatives, but lower concentrations of volatile phenols, terpenes, and C13-norisoprenoids compared to controls. These results suggest that temperature can modulate key metabolic pathways (phenylpropanoid and mevalonate) during berry development, and that the timing of heat exposure matters as much as its intensity. This work highlights the importance of early developmental stages for final berry quality and offers insights into how warming climates may reshape the flavor and aroma profile of wines in cool-climate regions.
