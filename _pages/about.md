I'm a Ph.D. candidate in Horticulture at the University of Wisconsin–Madison, where I study how climate warming affects the cold hardiness and dormancy of woody plants. My research sits at the intersection of plant physiology and climate change, trying to understand what a warmer world means for trees and crops that depend on cold winters to survive and thrive.

Growing up in Costa Rica, I never imagined I'd one day be measuring cold hardiness in boreal forests! But a deep curiosity about plants and how they respond to their environment took me from agricultural sciences at EARTH University in Limón, to a Master's in Plant Biology at Université Laval in Québec (where I studied how temperature shapes berry maturation and aroma in grapevines), and eventually to Madison, Wisconsin, where I joined Dr. Al Kovaleski's lab to tackle bigger questions about cold and warming at the ecosystem scale.

My dissertation focuses on the SPRUCE experiment, a whole-ecosystem warming project in a boreal peatland in Minnesota, where I explore how rising temperatures affect cold hardiness, dormancy, and freeze risk across overstory and understory species. The short answer? It's complicated, and that's what makes it engaging.

Beyond the lab and the field, I care deeply about teaching and mentorship. I've had the privilege of serving as a mentor for the Posse Program at UW-Madison, supporting students through their university journey. 

I'm always happy to connect, whether about research, teaching, or anything plant-related. Feel free to reach out at camposargued (at) wisc (dot) edu.
